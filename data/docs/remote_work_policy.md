# Remote Work Policy

## 1. Purpose

This Remote Work Policy explains how employees may work away from the office while still meeting business, security, collaboration, and confidentiality requirements. The company supports remote work where it helps employees work effectively and where the arrangement does not negatively affect service quality, team coordination, client support, operational coverage, or information security. Remote work is intended to provide flexibility, not to remove normal workplace responsibilities. Employees working remotely are expected to remain available, responsive, professional, and accountable in the same way as employees working from the office.

The policy is also designed to make remote work consistent across the organisation. Without clear rules, remote work can quickly become confusing, unfair, or risky. Some employees may assume they can work remotely whenever they want, while others may be unsure whether manager approval is required. This policy makes the expectations clear: remote work is allowed up to three days per week, but it must be approved by the employee's manager and must fit the needs of the team.

## 2. Scope

This policy applies to all employees, contractors, interns, and temporary staff who perform work for the company from a location outside the normal office environment. The policy applies whether remote work is regular, occasional, temporary, or part of a hybrid work arrangement.

The policy does not automatically permit employees to work from any location they choose. Remote work must be performed from a suitable, private, and secure location. Employees must not work from public places where confidential conversations, documents, screens, or customer information can be observed or overheard by unauthorised people. Locations such as cafes, airports, public libraries, shared lounges, public transport, or open hotel lobbies are not suitable for work involving confidential company information or access to internal systems.

This policy should be read together with the company's information security policy, acceptable use policy, data protection policy, code of conduct, health and safety guidance, and any department-specific attendance rules. If another policy sets a stricter requirement for a particular system, project, role, or client, the stricter requirement must be followed.

## 3. Remote Work Eligibility

Employees may work remotely up to three days per week with manager approval. Approval is not automatic and may depend on the employee's role, performance, team responsibilities, customer commitments, training needs, security requirements, and the ability to complete work effectively away from the office. A role that requires physical presence, direct equipment handling, in-person reception duties, secure document processing, facility access, or on-site technical support may have limited remote work options.

Managers are responsible for making fair and practical decisions about remote work requests. The decision should be based on business requirements rather than personal preference. For example, a manager may approve remote work for a software developer who can complete tasks securely from home, but may set stricter office attendance for a service desk employee who must support walk-in users. A manager may also require employees to attend the office during busy periods, team planning sessions, audits, client visits, system migrations, or training days.

Teams may set stricter office attendance rules for operational reasons. This means the general company limit of three remote days per week does not override a team's need for office coverage. If a department needs employees in the office on specific days to maintain service levels, support collaboration, or meet client obligations, employees must follow that requirement.

## 4. Request and Approval Process

Employees who want to work remotely on a regular basis must request approval from their manager before starting the arrangement. The request should include the proposed remote work days, the reason for the arrangement, the work location, the expected duration if the arrangement is temporary, and confirmation that the employee has a suitable workspace and secure internet connection. For occasional remote work, such as working from home for one day due to a delivery, appointment, or focused work requirement, employees should follow the normal team process for notifying and obtaining approval from their manager.

Managers should review each request by considering whether the employee's duties can be performed remotely, whether the employee can be contacted during working hours, whether the arrangement affects other team members, and whether there are any security or confidentiality concerns. Approval may be granted, rejected, or approved with conditions. Conditions may include fixed office days, mandatory attendance for certain meetings, limits on remote work during high workload periods, or additional security requirements.

Remote work approval may be changed or withdrawn if business needs change, if the arrangement is not working effectively, if the employee does not follow this policy, or if the employee's role changes. The company may also temporarily suspend remote work during incidents, major projects, compliance reviews, urgent operational periods, or other situations where office attendance is required.

## 5. Working Hours and Availability

Remote employees must follow their normal working hours unless a different schedule has been approved by their manager. Working remotely does not mean employees can work at random times or be unavailable during the business day. Employees must be reachable through approved communication tools such as email, phone, messaging platforms, video meetings, ticketing systems, and project management tools.

Employees should update their calendar and status when they are in meetings, away from their desk, on leave, or unavailable for an approved reason. Managers may set expectations for response times, daily check-ins, team meetings, handover notes, or work updates. These expectations should be reasonable and linked to the needs of the role.

Remote work should not reduce collaboration. Employees must attend required meetings, participate in team discussions, complete assigned tasks, and provide updates when needed. If an employee has repeated connectivity issues, misses meetings, is difficult to contact, or fails to meet work expectations, the manager may review or withdraw the remote work arrangement.

## 6. Security and VPN Requirements

Remote employees must use the company VPN when accessing internal systems. This includes internal applications, shared drives, administrative tools, databases, intranet resources, development environments, and any other system that the company has not approved for direct public access. The VPN helps protect company traffic and reduces the risk of unauthorised access.

Employees must only use approved company devices for company work unless an exception has been formally approved. Personal devices must not be used to store company files, process customer information, or access restricted systems unless permitted under a separate bring-your-own-device policy. Employees must not bypass security controls, disable antivirus tools, share passwords, install unauthorised software, or use unapproved remote access tools.

Passwords, multi-factor authentication codes, security tokens, and access credentials must be kept confidential. Employees must not write passwords on visible notes, save them in unapproved documents, send them by email or chat, or share them with family members, friends, contractors, or colleagues. If an employee suspects that a password, device, account, or VPN session has been compromised, they must report it to IT immediately.

## 7. Confidentiality and Private Work Environment

Work should be performed from a private location where confidential conversations and documents cannot be observed by unauthorised people. Employees must take reasonable steps to prevent others from viewing screens, hearing calls, reading printed documents, or accessing company information. Employees should use headphones for confidential calls, position screens away from windows and shared areas, and avoid discussing sensitive topics where others can overhear. If other people are present in the home or workspace, the employee must ensure they cannot access company systems or documents. Family members, friends, visitors, housemates, and contractors must not be allowed to use company equipment or view company information.

Printed documents should be avoided where possible. If printing is necessary, employees must store printed material securely and dispose of it using an approved secure disposal method. Confidential documents must not be placed in household bins, left on desks, or stored in shared areas. Employees must follow company records management requirements even when working remotely.

## 8. Company Equipment and Laptop Use

Company laptops must be locked when unattended and must not be shared with family members, friends, or contractors. Employees are responsible for taking care of company equipment and using it only for approved business purposes. Equipment may include laptops, monitors, keyboards, headsets, mobile phones, security tokens, chargers, docking stations, and other accessories provided by the company.

Employees must keep company devices secure at all times. Devices should be stored safely when not in use and should not be left unattended in vehicles, public places, shared spaces, or unlocked rooms. When travelling between the office and a remote work location, employees should keep devices with them where practical and avoid exposing them to theft, damage, or loss.

Employees must report lost, stolen, damaged, or malfunctioning equipment as soon as possible. Delayed reporting can increase security risks and may prevent IT from protecting company data. IT may remotely lock, wipe, or disable a device if it is lost, stolen, compromised, or no longer assigned to the employee.

## 9. Internet, Workspace, and Health and Safety

Employees working remotely must have a reliable internet connection that is suitable for video meetings, secure system access, file transfers, and normal work tasks. Employees are expected to arrange a workspace that supports safe and productive work. The company may provide guidance on ergonomics, safe workstation setup, screen positioning, lighting, breaks, and equipment use.

Remote work should not create avoidable health and safety risks. Employees should keep their workspace reasonably clear of hazards, use suitable furniture where possible, and take normal breaks during the day. Any work-related injury, accident, equipment fault, or safety concern that occurs during remote work must be reported according to company procedures.

The company is not responsible for all costs associated with a home workspace unless required by law or agreed in writing. Any reimbursement for equipment, internet, phone usage, or other expenses must follow the company's expense policy and must be approved before the cost is incurred.

## 10. Performance and Conduct

Remote employees are expected to maintain the same standards of performance, conduct, confidentiality, communication, and professionalism as office-based employees. Work output, deadlines, quality, attendance, responsiveness, and behaviour remain subject to normal management review.

Remote work must not be used as a substitute for personal leave, sick leave, childcare arrangements, or other leave unless approved by the manager in line with company policy. Employees who are unwell, unable to work, or responsible for significant personal commitments during working hours should discuss the appropriate leave or flexible work option with their manager.

Misuse of remote work arrangements may result in review, withdrawal of remote work approval, disciplinary action, or other action under company policy.

## 11. Review of Remote Work Arrangements

Managers should periodically review remote work arrangements to confirm they continue to meet business and team needs. Reviews may consider productivity, communication, service quality, attendance, security compliance, collaboration, employee wellbeing, and feedback from colleagues or stakeholders. A review does not mean the employee has done anything wrong. It is a normal way to ensure the arrangement remains suitable.

Employees should also raise concerns early if the remote work arrangement is not working well. For example, an employee may need better equipment, clearer meeting expectations, adjusted office days, or support with connectivity. Managers and employees should try to resolve practical issues before they become bigger problems.

This policy may be updated as business needs, technology, legal requirements, security risks, or working practices change. Employees will be notified of significant changes and may be required to confirm that they have read and understood the updated policy.
