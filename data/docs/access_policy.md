1. Purpose

This Software Access Policy explains how employees, contractors, interns, temporary staff, and authorised partners request, receive, use, renew, and lose access to company software systems. The purpose is to make software access secure, consistent, auditable, and based on genuine business need. Users should receive the access required to complete their work, but they should not keep access that is unnecessary, excessive, unclear, or outdated.

This policy supports three goals: timely access for approved work, proper approval before access is granted, and a reliable record showing who requested access, why it was needed, what level was approved, who approved it, and when it should end.

2. Scope

This policy applies to all company software systems that require a user account, role, permission, licence, token, group membership, service portal entry, or administrative profile. This includes productivity tools, customer platforms, finance systems, engineering tools, human resources applications, analytics dashboards, ticketing systems, cloud consoles, collaboration workspaces, and third-party hosted applications.


3. Access Request Requirement

Employees must request new software access through the IT service portal. Informal messages, personal emails, phone calls, hallway conversations, or direct requests to IT must not be treated as approved access requests. These channels may be used to ask questions, but the official request must still be recorded in the portal.

Each request must include four mandatory details: the business reason, the required access level, manager approval, and the expected duration. It must also identify the system, user, team, work activity, start date, and end date where relevant. Vague reasons such as “need access” or “for work” are not enough.

4. Business Reason and Access Level

The business reason must explain the specific work activity that requires the software. A good reason connects access to a job duty, project deliverable, support responsibility, reporting need, operational task, or approved training activity. For example, a reporting employee may need viewer access to prepare weekly dashboards, while a finance employee may need contributor access to process supplier invoices.

The requested access level must follow the principle of least privilege. This means the user should receive the minimum permissions needed to complete the approved task, not the broadest permissions available. Access levels may include viewer, contributor, editor, approver, reporting user, support operator, administrator, or system owner.

Requesters must not ask for administrator access simply because it is convenient. Managers must not approve broad access without checking whether a narrower role is enough. IT must provision only the approved level unless the request is updated and approved again.

5. Approval Requirements

Manager approval is required for all software access requests. The manager confirms that the access is appropriate for the user's role, project, team responsibilities, and expected duration. Approval must be recorded in the service portal or in an approval workflow linked to the portal. Verbal approval is not enough because it cannot be reliably audited.

Managers may consult specialist approvers when the request involves sensitive information, production environments, finance workflows, or privileged roles. If a manager is unavailable and access is time-sensitive, an authorised delegate may approve the request if the delegation is documented.

6. Duration, Renewal, and Expiry

Every request must include an expected duration. Access may be ongoing for a continuing role, fixed-term for a project, temporary for short work, or event-based for a specific activity such as audit support or release deployment. Ongoing access does not mean access is never reviewed. It means access is expected to continue while the user remains in the same role and the business need remains valid.

Fixed-term and temporary access should include an end date. Where possible, systems should automatically remove temporary access on the expiry date. If automatic expiry is not available, IT must track the expiry date in the service portal and remove access manually. Users and managers must request renewal before expiry if the work continues.

A renewal must confirm that the business reason still applies, the access level is still appropriate, and the extension is limited to the shortest reasonable period. If work ends early, the user or manager must notify IT so access can be removed.

7. Privileged Access

Privileged access is any access that allows a user to perform high-impact actions, view highly sensitive information, override normal controls, change configuration, manage accounts, approve critical workflows, access production environments, export large datasets, disable security settings, or perform administrative actions. Examples include system administrator, database administrator, cloud administrator, production support administrator, security administrator, payroll administrator, finance approver, and integration owner.

Privileged access requires approval from both the employee's manager and the system owner. The manager confirms the role-based business need. The system owner confirms that the privileged role is suitable for the system, environment, data sensitivity, and operational risk. Manager approval alone is not enough unless the manager is also the designated system owner.

Temporary privileged access expires after seven days unless renewed. This applies to privileged access granted for troubleshooting, deployment, audit support, incident response, migration, testing, emergency maintenance, or project-specific configuration. A shorter period should be used when the task can be completed sooner. Renewal must be documented and must confirm that privileged access is still required.

Where possible, privileged access should use named accounts, multi-factor authentication, session logging, approval workflows, and just-in-time elevation. Shared administrator accounts should be avoided unless a documented technical limitation exists.

8. Provisioning and User Responsibilities

After approvals are complete, IT provisions access according to the approved request. IT must confirm the user, system, access level, provisioning date, expiry date where applicable, and any security conditions. Standard roles, access groups, and templates should be used where available because they reduce errors and make reviews easier.

Users are responsible for using software access only for approved business purposes. Users must not share accounts, passwords, one-time passcodes, recovery codes, API keys, access tokens, administrator sessions, or privileged credentials with anyone else. Users must not attempt to access systems, dashboards, folders, records, databases, or environments outside their approved responsibilities.

Users must report access they no longer need or access that appears broader than expected. Reporting excess access is a normal part of keeping systems clean and secure.

9. Role Changes, Offboarding, and Reviews

Access must be removed or adjusted when an employee changes role, changes department, moves to another project, changes employment status, leaves the company, or no longer needs the system for their work. Access must follow current responsibilities, not historical convenience.

People Operations and managers must notify IT when a role change or exit occurs. For planned exits, access should normally be disabled at the end of the final working day. For urgent or involuntary exits, access may need to be removed immediately based on direction from People Operations, Legal, Security, or senior management.

Active access must be reviewed periodically. Privileged access should be reviewed at least quarterly. Standard access to sensitive systems should be reviewed at least twice a year. Lower-risk systems may be reviewed annually unless Security or the system owner requires more frequent review. Reviews should confirm that access is still needed and that the level remains appropriate.

10. Emergency Access, Exceptions, and Records

Emergency access may be granted when urgent action is required to restore service, respond to a security incident, prevent business disruption, correct a critical operational issue, or meet an urgent legal or compliance requirement. Emergency access must be limited to the permissions needed to address the issue and should be temporary by default.

If full approval cannot be obtained before urgent action is taken, the request must still be documented as soon as possible. The record must explain what happened, why access was needed, who authorised it, what access was granted, what actions were performed, and when the access was removed.

Exceptions to this policy must be documented and approved. An exception may be needed for a legacy system, technical migration, vendor limitation, or temporary outage of the normal approval workflow. Exceptions must include the reason, affected systems, affected users, duration, risks, and compensating controls.

The IT service portal is the official record for access requests, approvals, provisioning, renewals, expiry dates, removals, and review outcomes. A complete record should include the requester, user, system, access level, business reason, approvals, date provisioned, expected duration, renewal history, and removal date.

Failure to follow this policy may create security, operational, financial, legal, and audit risk. Corrective actions may include access removal, manager notification, mandatory training, system owner escalation, security investigation, vendor remediation, disciplinary action, or audit reporting. The goal is to protect company systems and keep access decisions controlled, fair, and trustworthy.

11. Policy Review

This policy must be reviewed at least annually. The review should consider changes in company systems, organisational structure, remote work practices, cloud platforms, regulatory obligations, customer requirements, audit findings, incident lessons, and technology capabilities.
