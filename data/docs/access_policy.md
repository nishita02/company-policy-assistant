# Software Access Policy

Expanded Internal Policy and Operating Procedure

Document Control

1. Purpose

This Software Access Policy explains how employees, contractors, interns, temporary staff, and authorised partners request, receive, use, renew, and lose access to company software systems. The purpose of this policy is to make software access predictable, secure, auditable, and aligned with genuine business needs. Every user should have the access needed to complete their work, but no user should hold access that is unnecessary, unclear, excessive, or outdated.

The company relies on many software platforms, including productivity suites, customer management tools, engineering platforms, finance systems, analytics dashboards, human resources applications, ticketing portals, collaboration tools, and cloud administration consoles. Each system may store business data, customer information, operational records, intellectual property, financial information, or employee records. Because of this, access to software is not treated as a casual request. It is a controlled business process that must be justified, approved, documented, reviewed, and removed when it is no longer required.

This policy supports three main goals. First, it helps employees receive required software access quickly when there is a valid work reason. Second, it protects systems by ensuring that approvals are collected before access is granted, especially for privileged access. Third, it creates a reliable record of who had access, why the access was needed, who approved it, what level of access was provided, and when the access should end. These records are important for internal governance, external audits, customer assurance, cyber security reviews, and incident investigations.

2. Scope

This policy applies to all company software systems that require a user account, role, permission, licence, token, administrator profile, shared workspace, service portal entry, or cloud-based access. It applies to systems used on company-managed devices, personally owned devices approved for work use, virtual desktops, cloud platforms, mobile applications, browser-based applications, and third-party hosted tools.

The policy applies to all users who access company systems, including full-time employees, part-time employees, contractors, consultants, vendors, interns, graduate trainees, and temporary workers. It also applies to managers and system owners who approve access, IT administrators who provision access, security staff who review access patterns, and People Operations staff who notify IT about role changes and employee exits.

The policy covers standard user access, elevated business access, privileged administrative access, temporary access, emergency access, renewal of access, removal of access, and periodic review of active access. It does not replace detailed technical configuration standards for individual systems. Instead, it sets the minimum process that must be followed before access is granted or continued.

3. Access Request Requirement

Employees must request new software access through the IT service portal. Requests sent through informal chat messages, personal emails, hallway conversations, phone calls, or direct messages to an IT administrator must not be treated as approved access requests. These informal messages may be used to ask questions, but the official request must still be created in the service portal so that the company has a complete record.

Each access request must include four mandatory details: the business reason, the required access level, manager approval, and the expected duration. A request that does not include these details must be returned to the requester for clarification before the access is provisioned. The IT team should not guess the business reason, select the access level without confirmation, or assume that access is permanent.

The request must identify the specific software system or application, the user who needs access, the team or department, the project or process that requires the software, and the date from which access is needed. Where the request relates to a client project, sensitive dataset, financial workflow, payroll process, production environment, or compliance activity, the request must include enough context for the approver to understand why the access is necessary and proportionate. Vague reasons such as “need access”, “for work”, “manager told me”, or “same as other team members” are not sufficient by themselves.

4. Business Reason Standard

The business reason must clearly explain the work activity that requires the software. A good business reason connects the requested access to a specific job duty, project deliverable, operational task, support responsibility, reporting need, or approved training activity. For example, a sales operations analyst may request read access to the customer relationship management dashboard to prepare weekly pipeline reports. A finance officer may request invoice processing access to complete month-end vendor reconciliation. A software engineer may request access to a staging deployment tool to support a release assigned to their team.

The business reason should also explain why the requested software is the correct system for the task. Where another existing tool already provides the necessary function, the requester should mention why the new access is still required. This avoids unnecessary duplication of licences and reduces the number of systems containing similar business data. The purpose is not to make the request difficult. The purpose is to make the access decision clear enough that someone reviewing it six months later can understand why the access was granted.

Managers are responsible for checking that the business reason is genuine and connected to the employee's duties. IT is responsible for confirming that the requested system and access level are technically appropriate. Security may review business reasons for sensitive systems, broad data access, unusual access patterns, or requests that involve privileged actions.

5. Access Levels

The required access level must be selected carefully. Access should follow the principle of least privilege, which means a user should receive the minimum level of access needed to complete the approved task. Common access levels include viewer, requester, contributor, editor, approver, reporting user, data analyst, support operator, power user, administrator, and system owner. The exact names may differ between systems, but the same principle applies in every case.

Viewer access may allow a user to read records, dashboards, reports, or documents without changing them. Contributor or editor access may allow a user to create, update, or submit records. Approver access may allow a user to approve transactions, workflows, changes, expenses, or user requests. Reporting or analyst access may allow wider read access to datasets for business analysis. Administrator access may allow the user to change configuration, create accounts, reset permissions, manage integrations, delete records, or view restricted logs.

Requesters must not ask for administrator access simply because it is convenient. Managers must not approve broad access without checking whether a narrower role is enough. IT must provision the approved level of access and should not add extra permissions unless the request is updated and approved. When a system does not support the exact level requested, IT must select the closest available role that does not exceed the approved business need, or escalate the issue to the system owner for a decision.

6. Manager Approval

Manager approval is required for all software access requests. The manager confirms that the request is appropriate for the employee's role, project, team responsibilities, and expected duration. Approval must be recorded in the IT service portal or in an integrated approval workflow connected to the portal. Verbal approval is not enough, because it cannot be reliably audited.

Before approving a request, the manager should check five points. First, the user is assigned to work that requires the software. Second, the requested access level is not broader than required. Third, the access duration is realistic and does not default to indefinite access without reason. Fourth, the request does not create a conflict of duties, such as the same user preparing and approving the same financial transaction. Fifth, the request is consistent with team responsibilities and any client or regulatory obligations.

Managers may delegate administrative coordination, but they remain accountable for approval decisions for their direct reports. If the employee is working across teams, the direct manager may consult the project manager, product owner, finance lead, or system owner before approving the request. When a manager is unavailable and access is time-sensitive, the approval may be provided by the manager's authorised delegate, provided the delegate's authority is documented.

7. Expected Duration and Expiry

Every access request must include an expected duration. The duration may be permanent for an ongoing role, fixed-term for a project, temporary for short work, or event-based for a specific activity such as audit support or release deployment. The expected duration allows IT and system owners to understand when access should be reviewed or removed.

Permanent access does not mean access lasts forever without review. It means the access is expected to continue while the user remains in the same role and while the business need continues. Permanent access must still be included in periodic access reviews. Fixed-term access should include an end date. Temporary access should include a clear expiry date and should be removed automatically where the system supports automatic expiry.

Users must not keep access after the work requirement has ended. If a project finishes earlier than expected, the requester or manager must notify IT so access can be removed. If work continues longer than expected, the user must request a renewal before the expiry date. The renewal must confirm that the business reason still applies and that the access level is still appropriate.

8. Privileged Access

Privileged access is any access that allows a user to perform high-impact actions, view highly sensitive information, override normal controls, change system configuration, manage other user accounts, access production environments, approve critical workflows, export large datasets, disable security settings, or perform administrative actions. Examples include system administrator, database administrator, cloud administrator, production support administrator, security administrator, payroll administrator, finance approver, and integration owner.

Privileged access requires approval from both the system owner and the employee's manager. The manager confirms the business need from a role and responsibility perspective. The system owner confirms that the requested privileged role is appropriate for the system, the environment, the data sensitivity, and the operational risk. Privileged access must not be granted based on manager approval alone unless the manager is also the designated system owner for that system.

The requester must provide a stronger business reason for privileged access than for standard access. The request should explain the administrative task, environment, affected systems, required permissions, expected duration, and any controls that will reduce risk. Where possible, privileged access should be granted through named accounts, role-based groups, privileged access management tools, just-in-time elevation, session logging, multi-factor authentication, and approval workflows. Shared administrator accounts should be avoided unless there is a documented technical constraint and compensating controls are in place.

9. Temporary Privileged Access

Temporary privileged access expires after seven days unless renewed. This rule applies to privileged access requested for troubleshooting, deployment, audit support, data correction, incident response, migration, testing, emergency maintenance, or project-specific configuration. Seven days is the maximum standard period for temporary privileged access. A shorter duration should be used when the work can be completed sooner.

The expiry date must be recorded when the access is granted. Where possible, the system should automatically remove the privileged role after seven days. If automatic removal is not available, IT must track the expiry date in the service portal and remove the access manually. The user and manager should receive notification before expiry when operationally possible, especially where the access supports planned project work.

Renewal of temporary privileged access requires a new approval or documented renewal in the original request. The renewal must confirm that the work is still active, privileged access is still required, and the requested extension is limited to the shortest reasonable period. Repeated renewal requests may be escalated to Security or the system owner to decide whether a different access model, role design, or operational process is needed. Temporary privileged access must not become permanent through repeated informal extensions.

10. Provisioning Process

After required approvals are complete, IT provisions the access according to the approved request. IT must confirm the user's identity, select the correct software system, apply the approved access level, record the date and time of provisioning, and update the service portal ticket. Where licence capacity is limited, IT may coordinate with the system owner or procurement team before granting access.

Provisioning should be completed using standard groups, roles, or access templates where available. Standard roles reduce manual errors and make reviews easier. Direct manual permission assignment should be avoided unless the system does not support group-based access or the request requires a unique configuration approved by the system owner. For sensitive systems, IT may require multi-factor authentication enrollment, device compliance checks, security training completion, or data handling acknowledgement before access is activated.

Once access is provisioned, the requester should receive confirmation that includes the system name, approved access level, expiry date if applicable, and any user responsibilities. The confirmation should not include passwords in plain text. Users must follow all security requirements for the system, including password rules, multi-factor authentication, acceptable use requirements, data classification rules, and restrictions on sharing accounts or exporting data.

11. Changes in Role, Department, or Business Need

Access must be removed or adjusted when an employee changes role, changes department, moves to a different project, transfers to another location, changes employment status, or no longer needs the system for their work. Role changes are one of the most common reasons for access risk because old access can remain active even after the user has moved to new responsibilities.

People Operations and managers must notify IT when a role change occurs. The notification should include the employee's new role, effective date, previous team, new team, and any access that should be retained, changed, or removed. The manager of the new role should submit new access requests for systems needed in the new role. The previous manager should confirm which old access is no longer needed. IT should update software permissions as close as possible to the role change date.

Access should not simply accumulate as an employee moves through the company. A user who previously worked in finance and later moves to sales should not keep finance system approval rights unless there is a clearly documented business reason approved by the relevant system owner. Similarly, a support engineer who moves away from production support should not retain production administrator privileges. Access must follow current work responsibilities, not historical convenience.

12. Employee Exit and Offboarding

Access must be removed when an employee leaves the company. This includes resignation, termination, contract completion, retirement, internship completion, vendor engagement end, or any other end of employment or engagement. Offboarding must be completed in a timely and controlled way to protect company systems and information.

People Operations must notify IT of the exit date and time as soon as the departure is confirmed. For standard planned exits, access should be disabled at the end of the user's final working day, unless earlier removal is required. For urgent or involuntary exits, access may need to be removed immediately according to direction from People Operations, Legal, Security, or senior management. Contractors and vendors must have end dates recorded when they are onboarded so access does not remain active after the engagement ends.

IT must disable or remove accounts, revoke active sessions where possible, remove privileged roles, collect or deactivate tokens, disable remote access, remove collaboration workspace access, recover company devices, and update the service portal record. Where a system is owned by a business team, the system owner must cooperate with IT to ensure the account is removed or disabled. Shared documents, project files, and business records created by the departing user should be transferred to the appropriate manager or team repository without keeping the former user active.

13. Periodic Access Reviews

Active access must be reviewed periodically to confirm that permissions remain appropriate. At minimum, privileged access should be reviewed quarterly, and standard access to sensitive systems should be reviewed at least twice a year. Lower-risk systems may be reviewed annually unless the system owner or Security requires more frequent review. Reviews may also be triggered by audits, incidents, organisational changes, system migrations, or changes in data sensitivity.

The review should list active users, access levels, managers, system owners, last login where available, privileged roles, temporary access dates, and users with unusual or high-risk permissions. Managers should confirm whether each user's access is still required for their current work. System owners should confirm whether the access level is appropriate for the system. IT should remove or adjust access that is no longer needed.

The outcome of each review must be documented. The record should show who reviewed the access, the date of review, actions taken, users removed, permissions reduced, permissions retained, and any exceptions approved. Reviews should not be treated as a checkbox exercise. They are a practical control for reducing outdated access, licence waste, and security risk.

14. Emergency Access

Emergency access may be granted when urgent action is required to restore service, respond to a security incident, prevent business disruption, correct a critical operational issue, or meet an urgent legal or compliance requirement. Emergency access must be limited to the access needed to address the emergency and should be temporary by default.

Where full approval cannot be obtained before action is taken, the request must still be documented as soon as possible. The user, manager, system owner, IT administrator, and Security team must record what happened, why the access was necessary, what access was granted, when it was granted, when it was removed, and what actions were performed. Emergency access should be reviewed after use to confirm that it was appropriate and that no unnecessary access remains active.

Emergency access is not a shortcut for poor planning. If a team repeatedly needs emergency access for predictable work, the system owner and manager must review the process and create a better planned access model. Planned releases, scheduled maintenance, audits, and migrations should use standard or temporary privileged access requests instead of emergency access wherever possible.

15. User Responsibilities

Users are responsible for using software access only for approved business purposes. Users must not share accounts, passwords, multi-factor authentication codes, access tokens, API keys, administrator sessions, or privileged credentials with anyone else. Users must not attempt to access systems, records, workspaces, projects, folders, dashboards, databases, or environments that are outside their approved responsibilities.

Users must report access they no longer need. They should also report access that appears broader than expected, such as administrator functions, financial approval rights, customer data exports, or production tools they did not request. Reporting excess access is not a problem for the user. It is a normal part of keeping access clean and accurate.

Users must protect any information they access through company software. This includes following data classification rules, avoiding unnecessary downloads, using approved storage locations, and not moving company information to personal email, personal cloud storage, unapproved messaging tools, or public websites. If a user suspects that their account has been compromised, they must report it immediately to IT or Security.

16. System Owner Responsibilities

System owners are accountable for the business use and risk profile of their assigned software systems. They define the available access roles, approve privileged access, support access reviews, confirm whether permissions are appropriate, and help IT remove or adjust access when business needs change.

System owners should maintain a clear description of the main roles available in the system. For each role, the description should explain what the role allows a user to do, what data the user can view, what actions the user can perform, and whether the role is considered privileged. This helps managers and requesters select access accurately and reduces back-and-forth during approval.

System owners should also support access monitoring. They should work with IT and Security to identify dormant accounts, excessive permissions, failed login patterns, unusual exports, shared accounts, outdated integrations, and privilege combinations that may create risk. Where the system stores sensitive customer, finance, employee, legal, or production data, the system owner must take extra care to ensure that access remains tightly controlled.

17. Record Keeping and Audit Evidence

The company must maintain accurate records for software access decisions. The IT service portal is the official record for requests, approvals, provisioning actions, expiry dates, renewals, removals, and review outcomes. Records must be retained according to the company's document retention schedule and any applicable legal, regulatory, or contractual requirements.

A complete access record should include the requester, user, software system, access level, business reason, manager approval, system owner approval where required, date requested, date approved, date provisioned, expected duration, expiry date, renewal history, and date removed. For privileged access, the record should also include the privileged role name, reason for elevation, approval from the system owner, and evidence of removal when access expires.

Audit evidence must be clear enough for an independent reviewer to understand what happened without relying on memory. Screenshots, approval logs, workflow history, group membership exports, review sign-offs, and removal confirmations may be used as supporting evidence. IT must avoid undocumented manual changes because they weaken audit quality and make it harder to investigate incidents.

18. Exceptions

Exceptions to this policy must be documented and approved. An exception may be needed where a legacy system cannot support individual accounts, a vendor platform has limited role options, a technical migration requires a temporary access model, or a system outage prevents normal approval workflow from being used. Exceptions must not be used simply to avoid normal process steps.

An exception request must explain the reason for the exception, the systems affected, the users affected, the duration of the exception, the risks created, and the controls that will reduce those risks. Examples of compensating controls include additional manager review, system owner monitoring, restricted time windows, session logging, manual reconciliation, multi-factor authentication, network restrictions, or daily access checks.

Exceptions should be temporary wherever possible. The owner of the exception must define an end date or review date. Long-running exceptions must be reviewed regularly and should be closed when the technical or business reason no longer applies. Security may reject an exception if the risk is too high or if safer alternatives are available.

19. Examples of Acceptable Requests

The following examples show the level of detail expected in access requests. These examples are fictional and are provided only to guide employees.

Example 1: “Requesting viewer access to the SalesInsight dashboard for Priya Mehta in the Revenue Operations team. Priya prepares the weekly regional pipeline summary for the APAC sales leadership meeting. She needs to view opportunity status, deal stage, region, owner, and forecast category. Viewer access is sufficient because she does not need to edit customer records. Access is required while she remains assigned to the reporting function.”

Example 2: “Requesting contributor access to the VendorPay application for Daniel Brooks in Accounts Payable. Daniel will process supplier invoices, attach supporting documents, and submit payment batches for approval. He does not require payment approval rights. Access is required from 5 July 2026 until 30 September 2026 while he covers the quarter-end processing workload.”

Example 3: “Requesting temporary privileged access to the staging deployment console for Anika Rao, Senior Platform Engineer. Access is required to configure deployment variables for Release 14.2 and validate the staging rollout before production approval. The access should expire in seven days. Manager approval and system owner approval are attached. Production administrator access is not requested.”

These examples are specific, practical, and limited. They explain who needs access, what system is involved, what level is needed, why the access is needed, and how long it should last.

20. Noncompliance

Failure to follow this policy may create security, operational, financial, legal, and audit risk. Examples of noncompliance include granting access without a service portal request, approving access without a valid business reason, assigning broader access than approved, keeping temporary privileged access beyond seven days without renewal, sharing administrator credentials, failing to remove access after role change, and leaving accounts active after an employee exits.

Noncompliance may result in corrective actions. These actions may include access removal, manager notification, mandatory training, process review, system owner escalation, security investigation, disciplinary action, vendor remediation, or audit reporting. The appropriate action depends on the nature of the issue, whether it was intentional, whether sensitive data was involved, whether the issue was repeated, and whether the user or team cooperated in resolving it.

The goal of enforcement is not to punish honest mistakes. The goal is to protect company systems and ensure access decisions remain controlled and trustworthy. Employees are encouraged to ask questions, report access issues, and correct mistakes early.

21. Policy Review and Continuous Improvement

This policy must be reviewed at least annually. The review should consider changes in company systems, organisational structure, remote work practices, cloud platforms, regulatory obligations, customer requirements, incident lessons, audit findings, and technology capabilities. IT, Security, People Operations, Legal, Procurement, and key business system owners should contribute to the review where relevant.

The company should continue improving the access process through automation, better role design, clearer approval workflows, faster removal of expired access, stronger privileged access management, and improved reporting. Where possible, systems should support single sign-on, multi-factor authentication, automated provisioning, automated deprovisioning, role-based access groups, expiry dates, approval logs, and review dashboards.

This policy becomes effective on the date listed in the document control section. All new access requests must follow this policy from that date. Existing access should be reviewed and aligned with this policy during the next scheduled access review or earlier where the access is privileged, sensitive, outdated, or no longer clearly justified.

Appendix A: Access Request Checklist

Software system or application name is clearly identified.

Requester and user are identified with department and role.

Business reason explains the actual work activity.

Required access level is selected and justified.

Manager approval is recorded in the service portal.

System owner approval is recorded for privileged access.

Expected duration or expiry date is included.

Temporary privileged access expires within seven days unless renewed.

Access removal is requested when role, employment, or business need changes.

All provisioning, renewal, and removal actions are documented.
