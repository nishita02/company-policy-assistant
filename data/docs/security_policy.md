# Security Policy

1. Purpose

This Security Policy explains how employees must protect company information, systems, devices, and accounts during normal business work. The purpose of the policy is to reduce the risk of data loss, account misuse, unauthorised access, accidental disclosure, and avoidable security incidents. The company stores customer records, employee information, financial documents, operational reports, contracts, product information, and internal communications. Because these records can be sensitive and business-critical, every employee must handle them carefully and follow the controls described in this policy.

Security is not only an IT responsibility. The IT Security team provides systems, monitoring, guidance, and incident response, but employees, managers, system owners, contractors, and temporary workers all play a role. A weak password, shared passcode, misplaced laptop, or file stored in the wrong place can create serious risk. This policy sets clear expectations so employees know where company files may be stored, how accounts must be protected, and when security concerns must be reported.

2. Scope

This policy applies to all employees, contractors, interns, consultants, temporary workers, and third-party users who access company systems or handle company information. It applies whether work is performed in the office, remotely, while travelling, or from a client site. The policy covers company-owned laptops and mobile devices, approved cloud systems, internal applications, customer databases, financial platforms, collaboration tools, email, messaging tools, and document repositories.

The policy applies to all company information, including draft documents, spreadsheets, reports, presentations, screenshots, exported data, meeting notes, customer details, employee records, credentials, recovery codes, operational metrics, and system logs. It also applies to temporary copies created for convenience. Downloading a report to a personal device, emailing a spreadsheet to a private account, or saving a file in a personal cloud folder is still covered by this policy.

3. Approved Storage Locations

Employees must store company files only in approved company systems. Approved systems include Microsoft SharePoint, OneDrive for Business, the company document management system, and other platforms formally reviewed and approved by IT Security. These systems provide access controls, logging, retention settings, backup support, and administrative oversight. These controls help the company protect information and recover documents when needed.

Employees must not use personal cloud services for company files. This includes Dropbox personal accounts, Google Drive personal accounts, personal iCloud, personal OneDrive, personal email inboxes, personal USB drives, and any other storage location that is not managed by the company. Personal storage systems do not provide the same visibility, access control, legal protection, or recovery process. Even when a personal service feels convenient, it creates unnecessary risk because the company cannot reliably manage the file.

Files should be stored in the correct business workspace or team folder. Employees should avoid unnecessary duplicate copies across different platforms. When sharing documents, employees should use links from approved systems rather than attaching copies wherever possible. This helps ensure that the latest version is used and that access can be removed when a person no longer needs the document.

4. File Handling and Confidentiality

Employees must handle company information according to its sensitivity. Confidential information must not be left visible on desks, printed and forgotten at shared printers, photographed without approval, or discussed where unauthorised people may hear it. Before sharing a file, employees must check that the recipient list, folder permission, or external email address is correct.

Employees should only access files that they need for their role. Having technical access to a folder does not mean every file in that folder should be opened, copied, or shared. If an employee receives access to information they do not need, they should notify their manager or the file owner so permissions can be corrected.

Screenshots, extracts, and exported reports must be treated with the same care as the original information. A screenshot of a customer record is still customer data. A spreadsheet exported from an internal reporting tool is still company information. These copies must remain in approved systems and must be deleted when no longer required.

5. Multi-Factor Authentication

Multi-factor authentication, also called MFA, is required for all systems that contain customer, employee, financial, or operational data. MFA adds an extra layer of protection by requiring more than a password before access is granted. This may include an authentication app, hardware token, biometric prompt, security key, or approved push notification.

Employees must complete MFA prompts only when they are personally signing in. If an employee receives an unexpected MFA prompt, they must deny the request and report it to IT Security. Unexpected prompts can indicate that someone else has the employee's password and is attempting to sign in. Employees must not approve MFA requests just to make the notification disappear.

MFA must not be disabled, bypassed, or moved to an unauthorised device. Employees who lose access to their MFA device must contact IT Security through the approved support process. Managers and colleagues must not help another employee work around MFA controls by sharing codes or using their own account.

6. Passcodes, Recovery Codes, and Credentials

Employees must not share one-time passcodes, MFA codes, recovery codes, passwords, security questions, authentication app approvals, or account reset links with another person. This applies even if the request appears to come from a manager, colleague, IT support representative, vendor, or executive. IT Security will never ask employees to reveal their password, one-time passcode, or recovery code.

Credentials must be kept private and must not be written on sticky notes, stored in unapproved documents, saved in shared spreadsheets, or sent through chat messages. Where a password manager is provided by the company, employees must use it for storing work passwords. Reusing the same password across work and personal accounts is not allowed because a breach in one service can expose access to another.

Shared accounts should be avoided wherever possible. Individual accounts provide accountability, audit logs, and proper access management. If a shared service account is required for technical reasons, it must be approved by the system owner and IT Security, documented, restricted to the minimum required access, and reviewed regularly.

7. Email, Phishing, and Suspicious Messages

Employees must be careful when opening links, attachments, invoices, shared documents, password reset emails, and requests for urgent action. Phishing messages may appear to come from trusted contacts or familiar systems. Warning signs include unusual sender addresses, unexpected attachments, spelling errors, unusual payment requests, fake login pages, pressure to act quickly, or requests to bypass normal approval processes.

Suspicious emails should be reported using the company's approved reporting method or forwarded to IT Security if instructed. Employees must not reply to suspicious messages, download unknown attachments, or enter credentials into websites opened from suspicious links. If an employee has already clicked a suspicious link, entered a password, approved an MFA prompt, or opened an attachment, they must report it immediately.

Employees should not use company email to register for personal services or use personal email to conduct company business. Keeping work and personal accounts separate helps protect company records and makes investigations, retention, and access management cleaner.

8. Devices and Endpoint Security

Company devices must be used in a secure manner. Employees must lock laptops and desktops when unattended, even for a short period. Company laptops must not be shared with family members, friends, visitors, or contractors unless the person has been formally authorised and issued appropriate access. Allowing another person to use a company laptop can expose confidential information, saved sessions, internal systems, and email.

Employees must not disable antivirus, endpoint protection, disk encryption, screen lock settings, browser protections, or automatic updates. These controls are there because attackers do not politely wait until everyone is ready. Software updates and security patches must be installed when prompted or according to IT instructions. If an update causes a problem, employees should contact IT Support rather than disabling security settings themselves.

Lost, stolen, damaged, or compromised devices must be reported immediately. This includes laptops, mobile phones, tablets, security keys, external drives, and any device that may contain or access company information. Quick reporting allows IT Security to lock accounts, wipe devices remotely, revoke sessions, and investigate possible exposure.

9. Access and Least Privilege

Access to company systems must be based on business need. Employees should only have the level of access required to perform their role. Managers are responsible for approving access requests and ensuring that access remains appropriate when roles, responsibilities, or projects change. System owners are responsible for defining access levels and reviewing sensitive permissions.

Employees must not use another person's account or allow someone else to use their account. Account sharing makes it impossible to know who performed an action and creates serious audit issues. Temporary access, elevated access, or access for a special project must be requested through the approved process and removed when it is no longer needed.

When employees change role, move teams, finish a project, or leave the company, access must be reviewed and removed where it is no longer required. Managers must notify IT or follow the appropriate workflow so access changes happen promptly. Access that remains active after it is no longer needed creates unnecessary risk.

10. Incident Reporting Requirements

Security incidents must be reported to the IT Security team within one hour of discovery. Employees should report concerns quickly, even if they are not fully sure whether an incident has occurred. Early reporting is more important than perfect certainty. Waiting until all details are known can make containment harder.

Examples of reportable incidents include lost laptops, stolen phones, suspected phishing, accidental disclosure of confidential information, emails sent to the wrong recipient, files shared with the wrong person, suspicious account activity, unexpected MFA prompts, malware warnings, unusual system behaviour, and discovery of company files stored in personal cloud services. Employees should also report situations where they believe a policy may have been breached accidentally.

A report should include the employee's name, contact details, affected system or device, time of discovery, description of what happened, people or files involved if known, and any action already taken. Employees should not delete evidence, wipe devices, forward suspicious emails widely, or attempt their own investigation unless instructed by IT Security.

11. Manager and System Owner Responsibilities

Managers must ensure their team members understand this policy and have access only to the systems they need. Managers must review access when employees join, move roles, take extended leave, or leave the company. They must also encourage prompt incident reporting and avoid blaming employees who report genuine mistakes.

System owners must identify which systems contain customer, employee, financial, or operational data and ensure MFA is enabled. They must maintain appropriate access groups, review privileged permissions, support audit requirements, and work with IT Security when risks are identified. System owners must also ensure that integrations, exports, and automated reports do not send sensitive information to unapproved locations.

12. Exceptions and Non-Compliance

Any exception to this policy must be approved by IT Security before the exception is used. Exceptions may be considered where there is a valid business reason and where compensating controls can reduce risk. For example, a project may require temporary use of a specific vendor platform, but that platform must be reviewed and approved before company information is stored or shared through it.

Non-compliance may result in access removal, mandatory retraining, manager review, disciplinary action, contract action, or other appropriate steps depending on the seriousness of the issue. The company will consider whether the breach was accidental, careless, repeated, intentional, or harmful. Employees who report mistakes quickly and cooperate with investigation and containment are helping the company manage risk.

This policy should be reviewed regularly to reflect changes in technology, business operations, legal requirements, and security threats. Employees who are unsure how to apply the policy should ask IT Security or their manager before taking action. When in doubt, store files only in approved systems, keep authentication codes private, and report suspicious activity fast.
